🚀 ARTEMIS

Deep but readable mission notes — concise, factual, and designed for curious minds.

Artemis II
Target: mid-2020s

Overview

Artemis II will be the first crewed mission in the Artemis program: a lunar flyby that validates Orion and crew systems in the deep-space environment. The spacecraft will travel to lunar distance and return, enabling astronauts to test procedures, life support, navigation, and communications before surface missions.

Launch Vehicle

This mission will launch atop NASA's Space Launch System (SLS) — currently the agency's most powerful operating rocket — pairing SLS with the Orion crew vehicle to send astronauts on a roughly 10-day mission around the Moon and back.

Key Objectives

Prove Orion's systems in a crewed, lunar-distance flight; verify life support and crew operations for multi-day missions; exercise navigation and communication links; and collect vital performance data that informs landing mission planning.

🛸 Orion 🚀 SLS 👩‍🚀 4 crew 🔁 Flyby

Notable Facts

Launch vehicle: Space Launch System (SLS), the most powerful rocket NASA is currently operating. Orion will carry a 4-person crew on a ~10-day mission to lunar distance and back. This flight will be the first crewed test of Orion in deep space.

Artemis III
Target: late 2020s

Overview

Artemis III aims to return humans to the lunar surface, landing astronauts near the south pole. The mission demonstrates surface operations, science sampling, and the use of a human landing system launched and staged from lunar orbit (with Gateway elements contributing to logistics).

Science & Exploration

Key work includes sampling permanently shadowed regions, studying volatile deposits like water ice, and demonstrating field techniques and power systems that enable longer stays on the Moon.

Notable Facts

Artemis III will employ a Human Landing System (HLS) to ferry astronauts from lunar orbit to the surface; the Gateway station is planned as an orbital waypoint. Focused on south-pole science, this mission will test extended surface operations and sample collection.

Artemis IV
Target: late 2020s–early 2030s

Overview

Artemis IV will expand crewed operations and begin deploying infrastructure modules and support systems. Surface stays grow longer and mission teams test sustained logistics and habitats.

Objectives

Deploy power systems, test resource utilization demos, and practice assembly/upkeep tasks that future missions will depend on for sustained presence.

Notable Facts

Artemis IV will begin delivering and testing infrastructure elements — power, communications, and habitat components — and will validate logistics chains needed for multi-week surface campaigns.

Artemis V & Beyond
Target: 2030s — roadmap through Artemis 8

Long-term Vision

After Artemis V, the program transitions toward a cadence of missions (Artemis 6, 7, 8...) that build the capability for sustained lunar operations: rotating crews, expanded science stations, and construction of surface infrastructure that supports long-duration stays.

Artemis 6–8 Highlights

Artemis 6 & 7 will continue to mature landing and logistics operations, supply the Gateway, and support multi-week surface campaigns. By Artemis 8 the program expects to routinely deploy habitat components and ISRU demonstrations, moving from short sorties toward the first sustained surface campaigns and more complex science objectives.

Path to Mars

The Moon serves as a testbed: life-support advances, radiation shielding, and In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) trials from Artemis missions directly inform Mars-bound architectures and crewed deep-space operations.

🌍 Partnerships 🏗️ Habitats 🔬 Science 🚀 Mars Prep

Notable Facts

Artemis V and the follow-on missions (6–8) emphasize ISRU demonstrations, rotating crews, and routine delivery of habitat modules. By Artemis 8 the program aims to support regular, longer-duration surface stays and robust science campaigns that feed directly into Mars mission planning.
