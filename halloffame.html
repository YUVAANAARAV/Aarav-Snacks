Astronaut Hall of Fame

Yuri Gagarin

Yuri Gagarin was the first human to journey into outer space on April 12, 1961. Born in Russia, he became a Soviet Air Force pilot before being selected for the historic Vostok 1 mission. Gagarin orbited the Earth once, reaching a maximum altitude of 327 km, and returned safely, instantly becoming a global icon for space exploration. His courage and pioneering spirit opened the door for humans to explore beyond Earth.

Neil Armstrong

Neil Armstrong, born in Ohio, USA, was the first person to walk on the Moon during the Apollo 11 mission on July 20, 1969. As the mission commander, he famously said, "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind." Armstrong had previously flown as a naval aviator and test pilot. His calm demeanor, precision, and leadership made him a symbol of human achievement in space exploration, inspiring millions around the world.

Buzz Aldrin

Buzz Aldrin, the lunar module pilot for Apollo 11, became the second human to walk on the Moon. A graduate of the United States Military Academy at West Point, he was a fighter pilot and aerospace engineer before joining NASA. Aldrin's work in orbital rendezvous techniques was critical to the success of lunar missions. Beyond the Moon, he has been a strong advocate for space exploration, writing books and promoting missions to Mars.

Valentina Tereshkova

Valentina Tereshkova, born in Russia, became the first woman in space aboard Vostok 6 in 1963. She orbited the Earth 48 times over three days. Before becoming an astronaut, she was a textile factory worker and an amateur parachutist. Her historic flight demonstrated that women could handle the rigors of space travel, and she became a symbol of gender equality in science and technology. Tereshkova later held political and advisory roles in Russia, promoting space education and science.

Alan Shepard

Alan Shepard was the first American in space, flying aboard Freedom 7 on May 5, 1961. Later, he commanded Apollo 14 and walked on the Moon, famously hitting golf balls on its surface. Shepard was a naval aviator and test pilot before joining NASA. His determination, skill, and contributions to astronaut training and lunar exploration left a lasting legacy in American space history.

John Glenn

John Glenn became the first American to orbit Earth aboard Friendship 7 in 1962. A decorated Marine Corps pilot and engineer, he later served as a U.S. Senator for 24 years. At age 77, Glenn flew again on the Space Shuttle Discovery, becoming the oldest person to fly in space. His dedication to public service and exploration made him an enduring figure in both political and space history.

Sally Ride

Sally Ride was the first American woman in space, flying aboard the Space Shuttle Challenger in 1983. A physicist and trained astronaut, she inspired countless young girls to pursue STEM careers. Ride co-founded Sally Ride Science, a nonprofit dedicated to education in science, technology, engineering, and math. Her calm courage and advocacy for science education cemented her legacy as a pioneering astronaut and role model.

Chris Hadfield

Chris Hadfield, a Canadian astronaut, became internationally known for commanding the International Space Station. He performed multiple spacewalks, conducted scientific experiments, and shared his experiences via social media and music videos from space. His outreach work has inspired millions worldwide to take interest in science and space exploration, and he continues to write, lecture, and advocate for space programs.

Peggy Whitson

Peggy Whitson holds the record for the most cumulative days in space by an American astronaut, totaling 665 days. She commanded the International Space Station twice and has conducted numerous spacewalks. Whitson's career as a biochemist and astronaut has been groundbreaking for women in STEM, demonstrating that dedication and expertise can lead to extraordinary achievements in space exploration.

Guion Bluford

Guion Bluford became the first African American in space in 1983 aboard the Space Shuttle Challenger. An engineer and fighter pilot, he contributed to space shuttle missions focusing on scientific experiments and satellite deployment. Bluford’s pioneering career broke racial barriers in NASA and inspired generations of scientists, engineers, and astronauts of diverse backgrounds to pursue careers in space exploration.

Mae Jemison

Mae Jemison was the first African American woman in space, flying aboard the Space Shuttle Endeavour in 1992. A physician, engineer, and scientist, she has dedicated her life to education and public outreach, promoting STEM careers for young people. Jemison has written books, given lectures, and participated in projects highlighting the importance of science, technology, and diversity in space exploration.

Michael Collins

Michael Collins piloted the Apollo 11 command module while Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walked on the Moon. An experienced pilot and engineer, Collins remained in lunar orbit, ensuring the spacecraft’s safe operation and the crew's return to Earth. His role was crucial to the mission’s success, and he has been remembered as a thoughtful, dedicated astronaut who valued teamwork and precision in space exploration.

Luca Parmitano

Luca Parmitano is an Italian astronaut who has flown two missions aboard the International Space Station and became the first Italian commander. He survived a life-threatening spacewalk when water leaked into his helmet, demonstrating composure under pressure. Parmitano continues to advocate for space exploration and inspires young people in Europe to pursue STEM careers and dream of spaceflight.

Laurel B. Clark

Laurel Clark, a physician and NASA astronaut, flew on STS-107 aboard Columbia in 2003. She tragically lost her life when the shuttle disintegrated during re-entry. Clark’s dedication to science, medicine, and space research continues to inspire future generations, and she is remembered for her courage, intelligence, and commitment to advancing human knowledge of space.

Tim Peake

Tim Peake is a British astronaut who flew to the International Space Station in 2015. As part of the European Space Agency, he conducted experiments in biology, physics, and human physiology. Peake became widely known for his educational outreach, including live lessons from space, spacewalks, and public engagement initiatives, inspiring young people across the world to explore science and technology.
